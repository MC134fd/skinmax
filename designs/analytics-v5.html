Version 5 · Editorial

Same 4 features, half the chrome

The SaaS-dashboard look is the tell that something was AI-generated. Gradients on every surface, emoji badges as decoration, cards nested in cards, delta chips shouting. This version strips all of that out. Typography carries the weight; color is used sparingly; copy sounds like a friend not an assistant. Closer to a magazine page than a product UI.

The design principle

Typography does the heavy lifting

AI-stamped designs lean on chrome — gradients, rounded cards, colored badges, drop shadows. A good human design uses one heavy typeface at 3-4 sizes and lets the words carry the hierarchy. That's the biggest single change here.

Nothing sits in a card. Sections are separated by thin 1-pixel rules instead. The eye moves down the page like it would a magazine article, not a Trello board.

Copywriting shift

Sentences, not stat blocks

v4 said "Your MVP: Avocado · +8.8 glow avg · 14 meals." That reads like analytics output.

v5 says "Your skin loves avocado. You've had it 14 times this month." Same data. The sentence frame does three things: makes it personal, implies ongoing knowledge, and — critically — sounds like something a human editor wrote, not an ML model.

Voice rules I followed
  • No "your [metric] is [delta]" — that pattern is AI-stamped.
  • Italic pull-quotes for warmth — "Your skin loves avocado."
  • Dates written out ("Thursday, April 10") instead of "Thu 4/10".
  • Observations, not announcements — "Your skin has taken its time" vs "Your glow is up 18 points."
  • Questions that invite a share — "Screenshot-worthy?" vs "Share my glow-up ✨".
Chrome removed

What I cut

  • All gradient backgrounds. v4 had 6+ gradient surfaces. v5 has one: the app background stays slightly warm. Everything else is flat.
  • All "card" containers except the phone itself. Content flows in one column, separated by thin horizontal rules. Mirrors how a newspaper article breaks sections.
  • All colored badge pills. Delta arrows (↑18) replace "+8 pts" chips. Numbers are smaller and quieter. The old pills were attention-grabbing at the cost of looking templated.
  • Emoji as decoration (the "✨" at the end of every button label). Kept only when it represents an actual thing — 🥑 for avocado, 🔥 for the streak tally, 💡 for a tip.
  • The "Share my glow-up ✨" primary button. Replaced with a text link at the end of the section — "Share this →". Less button, more magazine footer.
  • The big "78" with delta chip next to it. Replaced with a 72pt number anchored to a secondary line that reads "+8 from last month". Feels like a stat on a running-shoe page, not a dashboard widget.
Still here, just quieter

The 4 features survive

Every feature from v4 is in v5. I didn't cut scope — I cut decoration. Before/After is still the viral hero, just as two photos instead of a draggable slider. Glow Trend + Streak is still the retention anchor, just written as a sentence with a minimal chart below. Food → Skin Impact is still the USP, just opened with a headline. Cycle Sync is still the differentiator, just rendered as a thin progress bar instead of a lavender gradient card.

The data displayed is identical. The user can still see exactly what they could in v4. The only things I took away are the things that were doing the design's talking for it.

Tradeoff — be honest

When v5 is wrong

v5 is quieter. It requires more attention from the user to understand. If the target demographic expects the app to do the talking (a lot of 18-22 year olds expect this), v4 will convert better on first-open.

v5 wins if your audience is design-literate, pays for premium apps, and screenshots things because they find them interesting — not because the UI told them to. My read is that for a "Cal AI for skincare" positioning pitched at 18-35 women, v5 is the better shelf — v4 is the better ad. You might actually want the marketing to look like v4 and the product itself to look like v5.
